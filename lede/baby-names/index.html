What’s In A Name?

... a rose by any other name would smell just as sweet.

By Cherry Salazar
Published 14 July 2024

In 1597, William Shakespeare penned these immortal words for the tragedy Romeo and Juliet.

Through these lines, Juliet argued that names are mere identifiers and a poor reflection of a person’s character. Prevented from marrying Romeo because of a feud between their families, Juliet compares Romeo to “a rose by any other name.”

Shakespeare’s fascination with roses did not end there, for the flowers were again mentioned in his comedy A Midsummer Night's Dream — written in the same year as Romeo and Juliet but believed to have been written after it — and in The Two Noble Kinsmen, in which he wrote:

Of all flowers, methinks a rose is best.

Data from the U.S. Social Security Administration showed that while “Rose” is no longer among the most popular baby names, it continues to be an enduring option for parents.

Interestingly, a trend of gender-neutrality appears to begin to emerge. In 2016, 2017, 2020, and 2023, at least five male babies were named “Rose”.

The variation “Rosie” has also seen growing popularity in the past two decades. Meanwhile, “Rosa” slowly declined in the late 1990s and at the turn of the new millennium before steadying in the 2010s. The variation was also recorded for a few male babies in 1997, 1999, 2000, 2002, and 2003. •
